Heating FAQs

Everything regarding our emergency heat repair protocols.

Why does my heater smell like it's burning when I turn it on?

This is extremely common in Florida because your system sits completely inactive for essentially eight to nine months of the year. Dust and heavy debris settle deeply onto the electric heat strips inside your air handler unit. When the system finally fires up, it aggressively burns off that layer of dust, which creates a noticeable burning smell that should dissipate within roughly 30 minutes.

What exactly is a heat pump?

A heat pump is essentially an incredibly efficient air conditioner that has a mechanical reversing valve inside the compressor unit. Instead of pulling heat out of your house to cool it down, the reversing valve switches the flow of freon to pull ambient heat from outside and dump it aggressively into your home. They are phenomenally efficient for Florida winters because our temperatures rarely drop below freezing for extended periods.

Why is my heat pump suddenly blowing cold air during winter?

This usually indicates that the massive reversing valve inside your outdoor compressor has fundamentally failed or gotten physically stuck in the cooling position. Alternatively, your system might be lacking the proper freon levels required to execute the massive heat transfer process. You absolutely must call our technicians to diagnose this because running a crippled compressor will cause a catastrophic failure extremely fast.

Should I run my system heavily on "Emergency Heat"?

Absolutely not, unless your primary compressor has completely failed and the outside temperature is brutally cold. Engaging "Emergency Heat" entirely bypasses the efficient heat pump operation and violently turns on massive electrical resistance coils, operating essentially like a giant, energy-guzzling toaster. Your next monthly electricity bill will skyrocket to horrifying levels if you leave it running on this setting for long.
